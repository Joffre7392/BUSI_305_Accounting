What Is a T-Account?

A T-account is a visual representation of a ledger account. The name comes from its shape: a horizontal line with a vertical line creating a “T.” Every account in the general ledger can be represented this way.

Account Name

Debit (Dr)

Always Left

Credit (Cr)

Always Right

One side records increases and the other records decreases. Which side does what depends on the type of account.

Normal Balances & the Debit/Credit Rules

Accounts that sit on the left side of the accounting equation (Assets, Expenses) increase with debits. Accounts on the right side (Liabilities, Equity, Revenue) increase with credits.

Account Type Normal Balance Increase Decrease
Assets (A)DebitDrCr
Liabilities (L)CreditCrDr
Equity (SE)CreditCrDr
Revenue (R)CreditCrDr
Expenses (E)DebitDrCr

Key rule: In every journal entry, total debits must always equal total credits. This is the double-entry system — it ensures the accounting equation stays in balance.

The Expanded Accounting Equation

Assets = Liabilities + Common Stock + Retained Earnings

where RE changes from: + Revenue − Expenses − Dividends

Revenue increases SE (credit). Expenses decrease SE (debit). This is why revenue has a normal credit balance and expenses have a normal debit balance — they mirror their effect on equity.

Back to the Equation

The T-account system is a direct extension of A = L + SE. Accounts on the left side of the equation (assets) increase with debits (left side of the T). Accounts on the right side (liabilities, equity) increase with credits (right side of the T). Debits always equal credits — just like the equation always balances.

Why Do We Need Adjusting Entries?

Under accrual accounting, revenue is recognized when earned and expenses when incurred — regardless of when cash changes hands. At the end of each period, we make adjusting entries to align the books with economic reality.

There are four types, split into two categories:

Deferrals

Cash has already been exchanged — now we need to recognize the revenue or expense in the correct period.

1. Deferred (Unearned) Revenue

Cash received before earning it. Initially recorded as a liability because we owe the customer goods/services.

Example: Receive $6,000 on Nov 1 for a 4-month subscription.

At Nov 1 (initial receipt):

Cash6,000
Unearned Revenue6,000

Adjusting entry (each month, $1,500 earned):

Unearned Revenue1,500
Service Revenue1,500

Liability ↓ & Revenue ↑

2. Prepaid (Deferred) Expenses

Cash paid before using the benefit. Initially recorded as an asset because future periods benefit.

Example: Pay $3,600 on Dec 1 for 3 months of rent.

At Dec 1 (initial payment):

Prepaid Rent3,600
Cash3,600

Adjusting entry (each month, $1,200 used):

Rent Expense1,200
Prepaid Rent1,200

Asset ↓ & Expense ↑

Accruals

Revenue or expense has been incurred but cash has not yet changed hands.

3. Accrued Revenue

Revenue earned but not yet received in cash. We need to record the asset (receivable) and the revenue.

Example: Subletting office space for $500/month. Tenant hasn’t paid for December.

Rent Receivable500
Rent Revenue500

Asset ↑ & Revenue ↑

4. Accrued Expenses

Expense incurred but not yet paid. We record the expense and a liability (payable).

Example: Loan with 1% annual interest. $4,000 borrowed, 1 month accrued = $40.

Interest Expense40
Interest Payable40

Expense ↑ & Liability ↑

Back to the Equation

Every adjusting entry keeps A = L + SE in balance. Deferrals reclassify between balance sheet and income statement accounts (e.g., moving a liability to revenue or an asset to expense). Accruals create new balance sheet items (receivables or payables) alongside the corresponding income statement recognition.

The Trial Balance

A trial balance lists every account with its debit or credit balance. Accounts are listed in equation order: Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Revenue, Expenses. Total debits must equal total credits.

Unadjusted Trial Balance → Adjustments → Adjusted Trial Balance

After posting all regular transactions, we prepare the unadjusted trial balance. Then we post adjusting entries and prepare the adjusted trial balance. Financial statements are prepared from the adjusted trial balance.

Permanent vs. Temporary Accounts

Permanent (Real) Accounts

Balance carries forward period to period.

Assets, Liabilities, Equity (Common Stock, Retained Earnings)

These are the balance sheet accounts.

Temporary Accounts

Reset to zero at end of each period.

Revenue, Expenses, Dividends

These are the income statement accounts — they measure activity for one period only.

The Closing Process

At the end of each accounting period, we close temporary accounts by transferring their balances to Retained Earnings. This does two things: (1) resets Revenue and Expense accounts to zero for the next period, and (2) permanently updates Retained Earnings with the period’s net income.

Entry 1: Close Revenue Accounts

Debit each revenue account for its balance (to bring it to zero). Credit Retained Earnings for the total.

Sales Revenue75,000
Rental Revenue500
Retained Earnings75,500

Entry 2: Close Expense Accounts

Credit each expense account for its balance (to bring it to zero). Debit Retained Earnings for the total.

Retained Earnings59,740
COGS44,500
Salary Expense14,000
Rent Expense1,200
Interest Expense40

Net effect on Retained Earnings: +75,500 − 59,740 = +15,760 (= Net Income)

=
Back to the Equation

Closing is how Net Income flows from the income statement to the balance sheet. Revenue and expense accounts are temporary holding places. At year-end, their net effect (Net Income) transfers to Retained Earnings, permanently updating SE in A = L + SE. After closing, the only equity accounts with balances are Common Stock and Retained Earnings.
