Lectures 2–3 — The Financial Statement Effect Template (FSET) Approach

The Duality Principle

Every transaction affects at least two accounts. This is the fundamental concept behind double-entry bookkeeping.

When a company receives cash from a customer, for example, Cash increases but another account must also change — perhaps Revenue is earned, or a receivable is settled.

Key Takeaway

No single transaction can affect only one account. The accounting equation must ALWAYS balance after every transaction.

The Accounting Equation

Assets = Liabilities + Stockholders' Equity

Stockholders' Equity breaks into:

  • Contributed Capital — what owners invested
  • Retained Earnings — accumulated net income minus dividends

A = L + CC + RE — This expanded form is what the FSET uses.

The Financial Statement Effect Template (FSET)

The FSET is a tabular tool that shows how each transaction simultaneously affects the Balance Sheet and the Income Statement. Each row represents one transaction.

Transaction Balance Sheet Income Statement
Cash Noncash Assets Liabilities Contrib. Capital Retained Earnings Revenue Expense Net Income
Example: Earn service revenue, $1,000 cash +1,000 — — — +1,000 +1,000 — +1,000
=
Back to the Equation

The FSET is the accounting equation in action. Each column maps directly:

Cash + Noncash Assets = Liabilities + CC + RE

The Income Statement columns (Revenue, Expense, NI) track why RE changed. Every row keeps both sides equal. If you ever get stuck on a transaction, ask: "Which side went up? The other side must also go up, or something on the same side must go down."

Revenue → Increases RE

When a company earns revenue, two things happen on the FSET:

  • Balance Sheet: Retained Earnings increases (and an asset increases or a liability decreases)
  • Income Statement: Revenue increases, which increases Net Income

The RE column change always equals the Net Income column change.

= Equation Trace

Revenue ↑ → NI ↑ → RE ↑ (right side) → An asset must also ↑ to keep A = L + SE

Expenses → Decrease RE

When a company incurs an expense, two things happen on the FSET:

  • Balance Sheet: Retained Earnings decreases (and an asset decreases or a liability increases)
  • Income Statement: Expense increases, which decreases Net Income

Expenses are shown as positive numbers in the Expense column but reduce Net Income.

= Equation Trace

Expense ↑ → NI ↓ → RE ↓ (right side) → An asset must also ↓ (or a liability ↑) to keep A = L + SE

The Key Rule: RE Links the Two Statements

Retained Earnings on the Balance Sheet changes by the Net Income amount. This is the bridge between the Balance Sheet and the Income Statement.

Revenue Transaction

RE goes UP by revenue amount

Expense Transaction

RE goes DOWN by expense amount

Neither (e.g., borrow)

RE does NOT change; IS is blank

=
Seeing the System

Every topic in this course — inventory, revenue, depreciation, bonds, leases — is just a specific application of the same underlying system:

Assets = Liabilities + CC + RE

If you understand this equation and how transactions flow through it, you can work through any accounting problem. When you encounter a new topic, always ask: "Which components of the equation are affected, and how does it stay in balance?"

Practice Problem: Sunshine Consulting — First Quarter

This comprehensive problem ties everything together. Work through all 10 transactions, then build the financial statements from scratch and verify the accounting equation.

Setup

Sunshine Consulting begins operations on January 1. The following transactions occur during Q1:

  1. Jan 1: Owners invest $200,000 cash and contribute office equipment worth $50,000
  2. Jan 5: Borrows $80,000 from First National Bank (2-year note payable)
  3. Jan 15: Pays $24,000 for 12 months of office rent in advance (prepaid rent)
  4. Feb 1: Provides consulting services to Client A for $45,000 on account
  5. Feb 15: Receives $30,000 cash from Client A (partial collection)
  6. Feb 28: Pays employee salaries of $18,000
  7. Mar 1: Receives $25,000 cash in advance from Client B for services to be provided next quarter (unearned revenue)
  8. Mar 15: Provides $35,000 of consulting services for cash to Client C
  9. Mar 31: Pays utility bill of $2,000
  10. Mar 31: Records rent expense for Q1 (3 months of the prepaid rent = $6,000)
Transaction 1

Jan 1: Owners invest $200,000 cash and contribute office equipment worth $50,000

Cash Noncash Assets Liabilities CC RE Revenue Expense Net Income
+200,000 +50,000 — +250,000 — — — —

Journal Entry:

  Dr. Cash                    $200,000
  Dr. Office Equipment         $50,000
      Cr. Common Stock              $250,000
Running Totals: A = $250,000 | L = $0 | SE = $250,000 ✓

Cash and equipment both increase. The owners' total investment of $250,000 goes to Contributed Capital. Equipment contributed by owners increases both Assets AND CC — not just cash investments count.

Transaction 2

Jan 5: Borrows $80,000 from First National Bank (2-year note payable)

Cash Noncash Assets Liabilities CC RE Revenue Expense Net Income
+80,000 — +80,000 — — — — —

Journal Entry:

  Dr. Cash                     $80,000
      Cr. Notes Payable               $80,000
Running Totals: A = $330,000 | L = $80,000 | SE = $250,000 ✓

Cash increases and Notes Payable (a liability) increases. Borrowing is NOT revenue — it creates an obligation to repay.

Transaction 3

Jan 15: Pays $24,000 for 12 months of office rent in advance (prepaid rent)

Cash Noncash Assets Liabilities CC RE Revenue Expense Net Income
-24,000 +24,000 — — — — — —

Journal Entry:

  Dr. Prepaid Rent              $24,000
      Cr. Cash                          $24,000
Running Totals: A = $330,000 | L = $80,000 | SE = $250,000 ✓

Cash decreases but Prepaid Rent (a noncash asset) increases. This is an asset swap — total assets don't change. The $24,000 is NOT an expense yet because the benefit covers 12 future months.

Transaction 4

Feb 1: Provides consulting services to Client A for $45,000 on account

Cash Noncash Assets Liabilities CC RE Revenue Expense Net Income
— +45,000 — — +45,000 +45,000 — +45,000

Journal Entry:

  Dr. Accounts Receivable       $45,000
      Cr. Service Revenue               $45,000
Running Totals: A = $375,000 | L = $80,000 | SE = $295,000 ✓

Accounts Receivable (noncash asset) increases. Revenue is earned when the service is provided, not when cash is collected (accrual basis). RE increases through Net Income.

Transaction 5

Feb 15: Receives $30,000 cash from Client A (partial collection)

Cash Noncash Assets Liabilities CC RE Revenue Expense Net Income
+30,000 -30,000 — — — — — —

Journal Entry:

  Dr. Cash                     $30,000
      Cr. Accounts Receivable          $30,000
Running Totals: A = $375,000 | L = $80,000 | SE = $295,000 ✓

Cash increases but AR decreases — an asset swap. Revenue was already recorded in Transaction 4 when the service was provided. Collecting cash is NOT additional revenue.

Transaction 6

Feb 28: Pays employee salaries of $18,000

Cash Noncash Assets Liabilities CC RE Revenue Expense Net Income
-18,000 — — — -18,000 — +18,000 -18,000

Journal Entry:

  Dr. Wages Expense             $18,000
      Cr. Cash                          $18,000
Running Totals: A = $357,000 | L = $80,000 | SE = $277,000 ✓

Cash decreases. Wages Expense increases, which reduces Net Income and therefore Retained Earnings. Both sides of the equation decrease by $18,000.

Transaction 7

Mar 1: Receives $25,000 cash in advance from Client B for services to be provided next quarter

Cash Noncash Assets Liabilities CC RE Revenue Expense Net Income
+25,000 — +25,000 — — — — —

Journal Entry:

  Dr. Cash                     $25,000
      Cr. Unearned Revenue              $25,000
Running Totals: A = $382,000 | L = $105,000 | SE = $277,000 ✓

Cash increases but this is NOT revenue — the service hasn't been provided yet. The $25,000 is a liability (Unearned Revenue) because Sunshine owes Client B future services.

Transaction 8

Mar 15: Provides $35,000 of consulting services for cash to Client C

Cash Noncash Assets Liabilities CC RE Revenue Expense Net Income
+35,000 — — — +35,000 +35,000 — +35,000

Journal Entry:

  Dr. Cash                     $35,000
      Cr. Service Revenue               $35,000
Running Totals: A = $417,000 | L = $105,000 | SE = $312,000 ✓

Cash increases and Revenue is earned because the service is provided now. This increases RE through Net Income. Both sides of the equation increase by $35,000.

Transaction 9

Mar 31: Pays utility bill of $2,000

Cash Noncash Assets Liabilities CC RE Revenue Expense Net Income
-2,000 — — — -2,000 — +2,000 -2,000

Journal Entry:

  Dr. Utilities Expense          $2,000
      Cr. Cash                           $2,000
Running Totals: A = $415,000 | L = $105,000 | SE = $310,000 ✓

Cash decreases. Utilities Expense increases, reducing Net Income and RE. Both sides of the equation decrease by $2,000.

Transaction 10

Mar 31: Records rent expense for Q1 (3 months of the prepaid rent = $6,000)

Cash Noncash Assets Liabilities CC RE Revenue Expense Net Income
— -6,000 — — -6,000 — +6,000 -6,000

Journal Entry:

  Dr. Rent Expense               $6,000
      Cr. Prepaid Rent                   $6,000
Running Totals: A = $409,000 | L = $105,000 | SE = $304,000 ✓

Prepaid Rent (noncash asset) decreases by $6,000 — only 3 of the 12 months have been used. Rent Expense increases, reducing NI and RE. Cash is NOT affected — the cash was already paid in Transaction 3.

Key Teaching Points

1. Unearned Revenue is a Liability

The $25,000 from Client B is NOT revenue yet — it's a liability (Unearned Revenue) because the service hasn't been provided. Cash received ≠ Revenue earned.

2. Prepaid Expenses Convert Over Time

Only 3 months ($6,000) of the $24,000 prepaid rent is expensed — the rest stays as an asset (Prepaid Rent: $18,000). Prepaid assets convert to expenses as the benefit is consumed.

3. Collections are NOT Revenue

The $30,000 collection from Client A is NOT revenue — revenue was already recorded when services were provided (Transaction 4). Collecting cash just swaps one asset for another.

4. Non-Cash Investments Still Count

Equipment contributed by owners increases both Assets AND Contributed Capital — not just cash investments count. Any asset contributed in exchange for stock increases CC.

10 different transactions, all flowing through the same framework: A = L + SE. The equation balanced after every single one. Revenue increases RE through Net Income. Prepaid assets convert to expenses over time. Cash received in advance creates a liability, not revenue. This is the system.
